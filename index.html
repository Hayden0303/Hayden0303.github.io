Welcome to the world of mahjong — where ancient strategy meets timeless play. This isn't just a game with pretty tiles and clacking sounds. It's a cultural journey that spans empires, revolutions, and living room table battles.

Ancient Whispers & Mythical Origins - One popular legend claims Confucius invented mahjong around 500 BC, inspired by his love of birds — hence the name “mahjong,” meaning sparrow. - The three dragon tiles (Red, Green, White) are said to represent Confucian virtues: benevolence, sincerity, and filial piety. - While poetic, historians largely dismiss this tale due to lack of evidence before the Taiping era (mid-1800s). From Cards to Tiles: The Real Evolution - Mahjong likely evolved from Madiao, a Chinese card game from the Ming Dynasty (1368–1644). - Madiao used 40 paper cards with suits and numbers — very similar to modern mahjong tiles. - By the mid-19th century, mahjong emerged as a tile-based game, possibly created by: - Army officers during the Taiping Rebellion - A nobleman in Shanghai - Two brothers in Ningbo Rise in China: Qing Dynasty to Early 20th Century - Mahjong spread rapidly during the Qing Dynasty, becoming a favorite among aristocrats and commoners alike. - Played in tea houses, homes, and social clubs, it became a symbol of leisure and intellect. - Tiles were often made from bone or ivory, adding to its prestige.

Global Expansion & Western Adaptation - In the 1920s, American businessman Joseph P. Babcock brought mahjong to the U.S. from Shanghai. - He simplified the rules and published “Mah-Jongg: The Game of a Hundred Intelligences”. - Mahjong became a roaring fad, with themed parties and custom sets sold in department stores. - The game also spread to Japan, where it evolved into Riichi Mahjong, with unique scoring and rules.

From Paper to Bone: Origins of Tile Design - Mahjong began as a card game (Madiao) during the Ming Dynasty, using printed paper cards. - As the game evolved into tiles in the Qing Dynasty, artisans began crafting them from bone, bamboo, and ivory. - These materials allowed for carving and painting, turning each tile into a miniature work of art. - Early designs were inspired by Chinese currency, nature, and Confucian ideals — not just for aesthetics, but to embed meaning into gameplay. Suit Tiles: Symbolism in Everyday Life Characters (萬 / Wàn) - “Wàn” means ten thousand, symbolizing abundance and prosperity. - The calligraphy-style numerals reflect elegance and scholarly tradition. - This suit was likely chosen to represent wealth in large numbers, echoing the value of high-ranking officials and merchants. Bamboo (索 / Suǒ) - Represents strings used to bind ancient coins — a metaphor for connection and resilience. - The 1 Bamboo tile often features a bird (usually a sparrow or peacock), which evolved from early artistic interpretations of coin strings. - Bamboo itself is a symbol of flexibility and strength in Chinese culture. Circles (筒 / Tóng) - Modeled after ancient copper coins with round shapes and square holes. - Symbolizes commerce, luck, and financial success. - The circular motif was chosen to evoke the flow of wealth and the cyclical nature of fortune. Honor Tiles: Winds & Dragons as Cosmic Forces Wind Tiles (東, 南, 西, 北) - Represent the four cardinal directions, aligning with feng shui and Chinese cosmology. - Each direction carries spiritual meaning: - East: beginnings and spring - South: growth and summer - West: decline and autumn - North: endurance and winter Dragon Tiles (中, 發, 白) - Red Dragon (中 / Zhōng): “Center” — symbolizes success, power, and hitting the mark (like passing imperial exams). - Green Dragon (發 / Fā): “Wealth” — tied to growth and prosperity, often linked to the phrase 發財 (fa cai, “get rich”). - White Dragon (白 / Bái): “Purity” — represents clarity, honesty, and freedom from corruption. These tiles were likely chosen to reflect Confucian virtues and imperial symbolism, making the game a reflection of moral and cosmic order. Bonus Tiles: Nature’s Cycle and Cultural Icons - Flowers and Seasons tiles were added later to enhance gameplay and symbolism. - Each flower (Plum, Orchid, Bamboo, Chrysanthemum) corresponds to a Confucian ideal and a season. - These tiles celebrate natural beauty, time, and balance — reinforcing the idea that mahjong is more than a game; it’s a meditation on life. Artistic License & Regional Flair - Tile designs varied by region and artisan: - In 1920s Austria, tiles were inspired by Japanese woodblock prints. - In Germany and France, sets featured reptiles, birds, and even golf themes. - The #1 Bamboo tile became a canvas for creativity — morphing from a coin string into birds, peacocks, and cranes perched on bamboo. This artistic freedom turned mahjong sets into collectible treasures, each telling a unique story.

Singapore Mahjong Rules

  • Total Tiles: 148
  • Suits:Bamboo, Circle, Character (36 tiles each)
  • Honor Tiles: Winds (East, South, West, North – 16 tiles), Dragons (Red, Green, White – 12 tiles)
  • Flower Tiles: 8 (4 Seasons + 4 Flowers)
  • Animal Tiles: 4 (Cat, Rat, Rooster, Centipede)

Form a winning hand of 14 tiles consisting of:

  • 4 sets (Chow, Pong, or Kong)
  • 1 pair (called an Eye)
  • Meet the minimum scoring requirement (usually 1 or more “tai”)
  • Each player starts with 13 tiles (dealer starts with 14)
  • Flower and Animal tiles are immediately revealed and replaced from the back of the wall
  • Tiles are drawn from the wall in counterclockwise order
  • Chow: 3 consecutive tiles of the same suit
  • Pong: 3 identical tiles
  • Kong: 4 identical tiles (requires replacement tile)
  • Eye: A pair of identical tiles
  • Flower/Season: Bonus points; replaced immediately
  • Animal Tiles: Bonus points; Cat & Rat, Rooster & Centipede form pairs
  • Players take turns drawing and discarding tiles
  • Players may claim discarded tiles to complete Chow (only from previous player), Pong, or Kong
  • Winning tile can be self-drawn or claimed from discard
  • Priority for claiming discard: Win > Kong > Pong > Chow
  • Declare “Hu” when you complete a valid hand
  • Must meet minimum tai requirement
  • Special hands like 13 Wonders or 8 Flowers may auto-win
  • Basic Win: 1 tai
  • Self-drawn win: +1 tai
  • Pong of Dragon or Wind: +1 tai
  • All Pongs: +2 tai
  • Pure One Suit: +5 tai
  • All Flowers/Seasons: +4 tai
  • Animal pairs: +2 tai
  • Shooter Pays: Only the player who discarded the winning tile pays
  • Everyone Pays: All players pay, with shooter paying more
  • Common stakes: $0.30/$0.60 or $1/$2 per tai
  • Clarify house rules before starting
  • Respect discard timing and tile arrangement
  • Use chips or tokens for scoring in casual games
